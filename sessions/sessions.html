Activity: Stereotypes

  1. Label different papers with different cultures. Have the students grab a label out of a hat and pretend that they are what the paper says. Have the students hide their paper and introduce themselves to others in the classroom as if they were that label.
  2. Label different papers with different stereotypes about Americans and have the students grab a paper randomly, and re-introduce themselves around the classroom.
  3. Have the students draw out a representation of what they think people from a specific culture/ethnicity look like.

Discussion

  1. Reflection: ask the students if they enjoyed the game and allow them to say how they felt.
  2. What are stereotypes? Explain to the students how the first activity was reflective of stereotypes we have about people of other cultures and the second was of stereotypes that they have of us as Americans.
    • Make sure to ask students if they believe that they are any of the stereotypes presented about Americans?
    • IF they are not any of the stereotypes people have of Americans, do they really believe that other cultures can be stereotyped?
  3. Why is it not okay to stereotype people?

Instructions

  • What is intercultural communication? Intercultural communication is the way cultures communicate and understand each other.
  • Why is it important? Your entire life you will come in contact with people of different cultures and ethnicities and it is beneficial to know how to communicate with people everywhere. Also, if you decide to travel abroad it is so beneficial to know how to integrate and understand different cultures so you can truly enjoy your experience.
  • At the end of the session do the entry survey
  • Purpose of this session: To introduce the students to the topic of intercultural communication and deconstruct what they believe about people of other cultures.

Activity: Communal vs. Individual

  1. First, have all of the students pair up and grab one chair, have one student sit in the chair and have their partner gently pull the chair from under them.
  2. Have the partner count how long the student can stay in a sitting position without the chair, then switch positions when done.
  3. After that, have the students get into groups of four.
  4. Once in their groups have each sit facing each other interconnected and remove the chairs, have the group count how long they can remain in the sitting position without the chair.

Discussion

  1. Was it easier to remain in the position by yourself or with other people balancing and supporting you?
  2. Communal cultures: cultures that heavily depend on one another and have strong family connections.
    • Each person is encouraged to play a group sport and do what is best for society as a whole rather than themselves.
    • The rights of families, communities, and the collective supersede those of the individual
    • Laws and rules of these societies promote unity, brotherhood and selflessness.
    • Working with others and cooperating is the norm, everyone supports each other.
    • Strong and cohesive
  3. Individualistic: cultures that promote individuality and individual goals and achievement rather than the achievement of the whole.
    • Promotes individual goals, initiative and achievement
    • Individual rights are seen as being the most important. Rules attempt to ensure self-importance and individualism.
    • Independence is valued; less of a drive to help other citizens or communities than in collectivism.
    • Relying or being dependent on others is frequently seen as shameful
    • People are encouraged to do things on their own and to rely on themselves
    • Strive for their own success

Instructions

Purpose of this session: To teach the students the difference between individualistic cultures and communal cultures.

Activity: Time is Money

  1. Set up an obstacle course for the students and tell them they will get two chances to do the course.
  2. The first time, tell the students that they will have 5 minutes to complete the course, they can go to any obstacle they chose first but they have to do every obstacle before the five minutes are up.
  3. Say it is a competition and the person with the lowest time will get a prize.
  4. The next time, tell the students that they have 5 minutes to do the course but they can play whatever obstacle they want and they don’t have to do everything.

Discussion

  1. Did they have fun doing the activity?
  2. Who actually completed every obstacle the first time? How did they complete every obstacle?
    • Get the students to discuss if they completed every obstacle by planning and timing how long each obstacle would take?
    • Ask the students if they completed every obstacle the second time even when they did not have too? Why?
  3. The point of the activity: Monochronic vs. Polychronic
  4. Monochronic:
    • Like to do just one thing at a time.
    • They value a certain orderliness and sense of there being an appropriate time and place for everything.
    • They do not appreciate interruptions. They like to concentrate on the job at hand and take time commitments very seriously.
    • If you live in the United States, Canada or Northern Europe, you live in a monochronic culture.
  5. Polychronic:
    • Like to do multiple things at the same time.
    • A manager’s office in a polychronic culture typically has an open door, a ringing phone and a meeting all going on at the same time.
    • Though they can be easily distracted they also tend to manage interruptions well.
    • Willingness to change plans often and easily.
    • People are their primary concern (particularly those closely related to them or their function).
    • Tendency to build lifetime relationships.
    • Latin America, the Arabian part of the Middle East or sub-Saharan Africa, will be used to this behavior.

Instructions

  • Ask the students which time the students which time they enjoyed the activity the most?
    • If they say the first time, relate it to how the US is monochronic and we appreciate time-management and because of that we are highly competitive, ask the students if the competition made them want to go faster?
    • If they say the second time, relate it to polychronic societies and how they appreciate freedom and prioritize the person's mental health and freedom more than rushing them and confining them to time.
  • Ask students how time affects their lives? Which society would they want to be apart of?

Activity: What did you say?

  1. Split the class into two groups and have one stand in the hall and the other remain in the classroom.
  2. Tell the group in the classroom that they have to ask the other group to come with them to six flags.
  3. Tell them they have to stay a foot away from the members of the other group even when they are talking to each other, and to be direct and keep asking the other group members to go with them to six flags.
  4. Tell the group in the hall to keep trying to stand close to the other groups members and to say no they cannot go to six flags without saying “no I can’t go or don’t want to go”.

Discussion

  1. How hard was it to tell the other group no without saying no?
  2. Was it hard for you to understand what the other group was trying to say or did you understand they were trying to say no?
  3. High Context Cultures
    • Relationships build slowly and depend on trust. Productivity depends on relationships and the group process. An individual’s identity is rooted in groups (family, culture, work). Social structure and authority are centralized.
    • Nonverbal elements such as voice tone, gestures, facial expression and eye movement are significant. Verbal messages are indirect, and communication is seen as an art form or way of engaging someone. Disagreement is personalized, and a person is sensitive to conflict expressed in someone else’s nonverbal communication.
    • Space is communal. People stand close to each other and share the same space.
    • Everything has its own time, and time is not easily scheduled. Change is slow, and time is a process that belongs to others and nature.
    • Multiple sources of information are used. Thinking proceeds from general to specific. Learning occurs by observing others as they model or demonstrate and then practicing. Groups are preferred, and accuracy is valued.
    • Latin America, Africa, Southern Europe
  4. Low Context Cultures
    • Relationships begin and end quickly. Productivity depends on procedures and paying attention to the goal. The identity of individuals is rooted in themselves and their accomplishments. Social structure is decentralized.
    • Nonverbal elements are not significant. Verbal messages are explicit, and communication is seen as a way of exchanging information, ideas and opinions. Disagreement is depersonalized; the focus is on rational (not personal) solutions. An individual can be explicit about another person’s bothersome behavior.
    • Space is compartmentalized. Privacy is important, so people stand farther apart.
    • Events and tasks are scheduled and to be done at particular times. Change is fast, and time is a commodity to be spent or saved. One’s time is one’s own.
    • One source of information is used. Thinking proceeds from specific to general. Learning occurs by following the explicit directions and explanations of others. Individual orientation is preferred, and speed is valued.

Instructions

  • Ask which group do they think was high context and which is low context? And ask to explain reasoning. Explain which group is which.
  • Connect the cultural context with being polychronic or monochronic and individualistic and communal.
  • Ask students to think about which context they would want to live in and why.

Activity: You're not the boss of me

  1. Choose one student and tell the class that they are in charge, have the student gather the class and do a game of ships and sailors.
  2. After that have the student tell the group to go back to their seats and be quiet and wait for further instruction.

Discussion

  1. Ask the students how they felt having another student tell them what to do? Why did they feel that way?
  2. Ask the student how they felt instructing the class?
  3. High Power Distance
    • Countries that have high power distance include many Latin American, African and Asian countries.
    • Hierarchy is an essential part of the society, and unequal distribution of power is seen as beneficial.
    • Those higher on the hierarchy are expected to take care of those lower on the hierarchy, and it is often appropriate for those in power to make decisions without consulting their subordinates.
    • Those lower on the hierarchy generally are expected to obey their superiors. For instance, students may not be allowed to argue with their professors, and it may not be acceptable for employers to disagree with their boss.
  4. Lower Power Distance
    • Countries with low power distance include Israel, Denmark, Ireland and Austria
    • Members of the society value equality and democracy,
    • More acceptable for those who are junior in age or rank to question authority. For instance, students in schools and universities are encouraged to express opinions freely and disagree with their professors, while decisions in organizations are often reached by taking into account opinions of all employers.
  5. Ask what they think the United States is?
    • Surprisingly the US would be considered low context. Why do you think that is?
    • Which would you like to be a part of?

Instructions

Now the students will know all of the dynamics of culture, the next fundamental step will be teaching the students different cultures so that they can apply the different dynamics and see the beauty in cultural differences.
